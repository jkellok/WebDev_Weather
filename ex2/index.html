Riichi Mahjong

Riichi Mahjong, also called Japanese Mahjong, is a Japanese variation of Chinese Mahjong. It is a four-player table game played with mahjong tiles. It is very popular in Japan, as Japan has thousands of mahjong parlors. There are also mobile apps and browser games where you can play against the computer or other people online.

Mahjong Tiles

The game is played with 136 tiles. There are 34 different kinds of tiles, four of each kind. The tiles are grouped to suits and honor tiles. Suit tiles consist of numbers 1-9.

Suit tiles

Pinzu

Pin tiles consist of circles.

Souzu

Sou tiles consist of bamboo sticks but 1-sou is a bird.

Manzu

Man tiles consist of characters for ten thousands on the lower half and characters for the tile number ranging from 1-9.

Honor tiles

Wind

There are four wind tiles: East, South, West and North

Dragon

There are three dragon tiles: White, Green and Red.

The Hand

Each player has 13 tiles in their hand and at the start of their turn they draw a tile from the wall. The player temporarily has 14 tiles and if the player doesn't declare a win, they must discard one tile from their hand.

The winning hand consists of four sets of three tiles and a pair (3+3+3+3+2=14). The winning hand must also have a valid yaku, which can be thought as a winning condition.

Different sets of tiles

  • Sequence consists of three consecutive tiles of the same suit, for example 4-5-6 or 7-8-9.
  • Triplet consists of three of the same tile.
  • Quads are usually called kan. A kan consist of four of the same tiles. It needs to be declared to form a part of a winning hand. When declared, the player receives an extra tile from the dead wall because otherwise they would have one tile less.

Calling tiles

The player can also call other players discards when the tile is discarded. The player must form a set with the tile and display it face up on the table. This makes the hand an open hand, which can lessen the value of the hand. Players can also choose not to call tiles and keep their hand closed. That player continues the game and must discard a tile from their hand. There are different calls.

  • Chii is called out when a player can make a sequence with the discarded tile. The discard must be from the player before their turn i.e. from the left player because the turns go anticlockwise.
  • Pon is called out when a player can make a triplet with the discarded tile. The discard can be from any player. If two players want the same discard, pon has precedence.
  • Kan is called when the player has four identical tiles. Open quad is when the kan is formed of another player's discard. You can make an added open quad by adding a tile from your own hand to your pon. You can also have a closed quad. In this case all the four tiles are from your hand. This does not make the hand open.

Winning

When player is one tile from winning, the hand is said to be in tenpai. The player only needs one tile to declare their win. If they win on other player's discard, they call "ron" and reveal their hand. The player who discarded the tile must pay the winner the amount of points that the winning hand is worth. If they win by drawing the winning tile, the player calls "tsumo" and reveals their hand. All the other players must pay the winner. The pay is shared, for example if the hand is worth 3000 points, the players each pay 1000 points to the winner.

Yaku

The winning hand must have a yaku. Yaku is determined by the shape of the hand or different conditions. Each yaku has its own han value. Hands worth 1 han are easier to get than hands worth multiple han. In some cases an open hand will get -1 han (2 han - 1 = 1 han). Yakuman is a value for hands that are very hard to get. Yakuman hands ignore normal scoring patterns. The Wikipedia article has a good and a comprehensive list of different yaku.
